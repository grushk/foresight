About Us

Our methodological approach is described in the best-selling textbook The Art & Science of Technology Transfer (Wiley, 2006) by our CEO Phyl Speser, J.D., Ph.D. At the heart of the method is the centrality of understand the needs of the people who will use a technology, which we call the end-users. After all, if there is no end-user willing to buy the technology it is hard to see how it can be commercialized. The technology is examined to determine its performance and the features and functionalities which create that performance. (The way these features and functionalities also created underlies the patent claims for the technology). They are mapped to potential needs in the market place. As potential end-users are located, the performance is more tightly analyzed against end-user requirements to see if a competitive opening exists. Next we look at the ease-of-use and price desired by end-users to see if the technology can meet those requirements. If so, a potential competitive advantage exists. Of course, we need to look at market barriers and drivers in order to determine if this is a sustainable competitive advantage, as especially with early stage technologies, market entry can be several years downstream. Where there is a competitive advantage, we find the potential commercialization partner (licensee, customer, alliance partner, funding agency, etc.) by backtracking up the supply chain from the end-user to appropriate commercialization partner for the technology being analyzed. The Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats method is used to determine launch tactics. Value is determined using net discounted cash flow analysis and real options.
